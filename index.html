I Introduction

Our group is developing a toolkit to support literary scholars. The toolkit, called lit_tk, has the following features:

  1. It supports a style of digital research called Exploratory Programming. It uses the jupyter notebook as the foundation for exploration.

  2. It provides a programming library, currently in Python and in the future in R, that can be simply imported into the jupyter environment.

  3. It focuses on the basic tasks in an exploration of a literary research question: obtaining data, cleaning data, exploring correlations, building predictive models.

III Logistics and Timelines

The ADDS graduate certificate will be offered online. The slate of courses will not only provide students with opportunities to study digital content using a digital platform, but would give them experience using and thinking through real-world applications for their work. Platforms like Canvas are designed to provide storage space for housing papers, email discussions, and syllabi. They are largely meant to be invisible backdrops or repositories for course content. In sharp contrast, new platforms like Slack (see short video below) are designed to facilitate live conversation and interactive learning. The ADDS certificate would make good use of leading course delivery platforms that foreground collaborative opportunities and facilitate exceptional student engagement opportunities.



Sample use of Slack (1 minute)



Course development timeline (see table below): We propose to bring courses online in an incremental process. Our strategy is to test each course out in a hybrid fashion before moving fully online. Fickas has used this strategy successfully with undergrad CS courses; he won a UO Williams Fellowship in 2015 based on the inventiveness of this approach. The first course is hybrid in that only local students will be allowed to enroll, and instructor help will be available for face-to-face meetings with students. At the conclusion of the hybrid course, Instructors will craft an online help and discussion component for the online version of the course based on their experiences and student responses to the hybrid course.

We have also included a row for adding additional courses to the ADDS certificate in summer 2019 to support a full Master's program, which is part of our future plans.

The full certificate program will be ready by summer 2019. With each first offering, we will simultaneously submit a course-approval form to the Graduate School Curriculum Committee.

Instructors will design courses to be fully accessible outside of the certificate trajectory, thereby allowing students to enroll in individual courses without the obligation to pursue a certificate. Looking at the courses separately:

  • Non-major computer science courses remain in demand on campus. The CIS department enrolls roughly 1000 non-majors during the academic year. Courses 2 and 3 are computer science courses that will be designed to allow interested graduate students to take both courses outside of the ADDS certificate. Again using CIS courses as a basis, we expect that many non-certificate students will switch to the certificate track once they are hooked by courses 2 and 3.

  • Course 4 is crucial to the work being done at universities around the world. Like courses 2 and 3, Course 4 could be taken separately from the certificate program for those interested in learning basic skills in digital scholarship.

  • Courses 1 and 5 give the Certificate coherence and coalescence. Enrollment depends on our ability to attract students to the ADDS program.

  • Course 6 is an elective course. We will encourage new elective courses to be developed by offering faculty stipends. These new online courses, in turn, can attract a large and worldwide audience.
